Your YouTube Reading List

A curated study guide of creators worth watching, deconstructing, and learning from. Each one teaches a different lesson about what makes YouTube work.

Prepared for Abel Raberin · May 2026 · Harry Verity

Why this list exists

YouTube is a visual platform. The information alone isn't enough — it's your unique perspective, your life, and how you share it that makes people stay. Every creator in this guide was chosen because they teach a specific lesson about what works on YouTube.

Two stats matter above all else on YouTube:

CTR
Click-Through Rate — the packaging: title, thumbnail & text
AVD
Average View Duration — how long viewers stay as a % of total

If viewers click off after 30 seconds, your video hasn't hooked them in or delivered on the promise of the packaging. Study these creators with that lens.

01

Codie Sanchez

2.17M subscribers 391M views @CodieSanchezCT

Codie is the proof that YouTube isn't just about information — it's about who you are. She's a former journalist who did segments for Fox Business. She's married to an ex-Navy SEAL, and you can clearly see that fitness and discipline are part of what drives her. She films everywhere — in the car, at the gym, in front of a whiteboard. Every video is part informational, part her life.

Her contrarian approach is what makes her stand out. She takes accepted wisdom and flips it. She's not just presenting facts — she's debunking the things everyone takes for granted and presenting her unique, hard-hitting perspective.

Key lesson
Your videos need your unique spin. It's not just the information — it's your life, your background, your perspective woven into it. That's what makes you irreplaceable. Codie's journalism training, her Navy SEAL husband's discipline, her business experience — all of it feeds into a voice nobody else can replicate.

02

Matt Bevan — If You're Listening

ABC News In-depth · 2.34M subs 1B+ views @ABCNewsIndepth

Another journalist — notice the pattern. Matt hosts "If You're Listening" for ABC Australia. His videos take the big themes of the day — the Epstein scandal, the Iran war — and use decades of archival footage from ABC's archive to go back in time and explain them. He's funny, and that personality comes through strongly.

His USP is the archive from ABC Australia which spans decades. Clipping existing footage is a powerful way to make your videos more interesting without needing a huge production budget. He also never runs out of ideas because he follows the news cycle and uses current events as a peg to tell a wider story.

Key lesson
Use current events as a peg to tell a wider story. There's been XYZ story happen in crypto — SBF is arrested, Binance CEO is arrested, Logan Paul sues Coffeezilla — then pivot into your core message. The news cycle is an infinite content engine. Clipping and archival footage makes your videos richer without filming everything from scratch.

Coffeezilla

4.53M subscribers 574M views @Coffeezilla

Coffeezilla does deep investigative journalism — next-level exposés of the crypto world with a very distinctive take. He's "the internet detective." His main channel is polished, heavily researched, and cinematic. But here's the interesting part...

He has a second channel called Voidzilla (1.55M subs) that is extremely basic — just him with a black background talking to camera, occasional whiteboarding — and it still gets huge views. This proves that production quality isn't everything. Think about your setting and what makes you unique.

Key lesson
Two channels, two approaches, both work. The main channel teaches you about investigative depth and distinctive branding. Voidzilla teaches you that a person talking authentically to camera with zero production can still pull 1.5M subscribers. What matters is the person and the perspective, not the set.

Alex Hormozi

4.17M subscribers 984M views @AlexHormozi

Hormozi is focused entirely on the audience and the value he brings — largely about how you can make more money. He has high authority and uses hooks like "If I was starting all over again I would do this" — this works for him because he has the clout to back it up.

You don't have that clout yet. But some of his strategy still transfers: give away massive value upfront, hold nothing back, and then sell individualised 1-to-1 coaching for people who want to spend time with you. The free content is the funnel.

Key lesson
Huge value upfront, give away all the secrets, then sell 1-to-1 coaching. The strategy works at any scale. The authority hooks ("If I was starting over...") work once you've earned the credibility. For now, focus on the value-first approach — it compounds.

Ali Abdaal

6.59M subscribers 542M views @aliabdaal

Ali is one of the most-studied YouTube creators in the productivity/business space. He started as a junior doctor sharing study tips and evolved into one of the biggest channels on the platform. His production quality is consistently high, his titles are optimised, and he's mastered the art of the "How I'd do X if I started over" format.

Key lesson
Consistency, iteration, and evolution. Ali didn't start where he is now. He refined his format, titles, and thumbnails over hundreds of videos. Study how his style has evolved from early medical school content to polished business advice — the growth trajectory is the lesson itself.

Nischa

2.18M subscribers 133M views @nischa

Nischa is a former investment banker and chartered accountant who makes videos about accumulating wealth from a 9-to-5 and achieving financial freedom. The biggest lesson from her channel is the way she leverages her professional expertise to position herself as a credible authority.

She doesn't pretend to be a flashy entrepreneur — she's an accountant who happens to know how money works, and she shares that knowledge clearly. Her titles follow the "Accountant Explains:" format which immediately establishes trust.

Key lesson
Leverage your background. Your expertise is your positioning. Nischa doesn't need to be loud or contrarian — her professional credentials do the heavy lifting. Whatever your background in crypto/finance, lean into it hard. "Former X explains..." is an incredibly powerful title format.

07

Andrew Gold — Heretics

779K subscribers 85.7M views @andrewgoldheretics

Andrew Gold has some of the best hooks in the business. He creates a trailer for each episode by clipping the most controversial moments from the podcast and overlaying them with previous media appearances or a bit of backstory. It's masterful packaging.

While this is a podcast and not in your niche, it's worth studying how the interview is structured and how those viral moments are extracted to make the trailer. Stephen Bartlett's Diary of a CEO (16.3M subs) does something very similar.

Key lesson
Master the hook. The first 30 seconds decide whether someone stays. Study how Gold clips the most explosive, controversial, curiosity-provoking moments and front-loads them. It's harder to apply this to a 10-minute video than a 2-hour podcast — but the principle of leading with your most compelling content applies everywhere.

08

Uptin

264K subscribers 119M views @uptin

Another fellow journalist — Uptin is a former CNBC journalist. He takes a journalist's inquisitive approach without really taking a side. His videos come across as travel vlogs more often than not. He goes out to meet someone or do something — he doesn't just do piece to camera, he films on his own camera, which makes him seem more authentic.

He uses lots of B-roll. The production feels real and on-the-ground rather than studio-polished. Videos like "I visited McDonald's in 21 countries" or "Why Are People Leaving California?" combine curiosity, travel, and storytelling into something that feels genuine.

Key lesson
Get out of the studio. Filming on location, meeting real people, and showing your journey makes content feel authentic. You don't need a fancy set — you need to go somewhere interesting and bring the viewer along. B-roll is your friend.

General Viewing

Study YouTube as broadly as possible

Don't just watch creators in your niche. Study YouTube as a platform. Watch all kinds — even the guilty pleasures you binge-watch. You'll learn more about hooks, pacing, and retention from a channel that keeps you glued to the screen than from any course.

Guilty Pleasures That Teach You Retention

Channels like Midwest Safety (police body cam compilations) and Auditing Britain keep viewers watching for 20+ minutes. Ask yourself why. What's the hook? How do they maintain tension? How do they structure a narrative arc within each clip? These are retention lessons hiding in plain sight.

Remember
The two numbers that matter:

1. Click-Through Rate (CTR) — this is your packaging: title, thumbnail, and the text on the thumbnail. If nobody clicks, nobody watches.

2. Average View Duration (%) — if viewers click off after 30 seconds, your video hasn't hooked them in or delivered on the promise of the packaging. These two metrics are the entire game.
